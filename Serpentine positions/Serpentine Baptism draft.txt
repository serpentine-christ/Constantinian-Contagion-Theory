Serpentine Baptism: Water and Fire


1. The tradition the Serpentine Church inherits records two descents in the life of the figure at its center. The first at the Jordan, water and dove. The second in the wilderness, after forty days, fire. The institutional churches preserved the first and lost the second. This document recovers both, names them correctly, and rejects the cargo-cult apparatus that has been built around the first in place of the thing itself.

2. Baptism is the direct encounter with the Shekinah. The descent of the Holy Spirit. The meeting with the divine indwelling — with what was always within and is now recognized as within. Nothing else is baptism.

3. The Shekinah is the name the Hebrew tradition gives to the divine presence that dwells with and within. The Christian tradition calls the same reality the Holy Spirit. The Hindu tradition speaks of atman recognizing brahman, or the descent of shakti. The Buddhist traditions speak in the vocabulary of recognition of the nature of mind. The Sufis speak of fana and baqa. The names are many. The encounter is one.

4. The contact is the only thing that matters. Nothing else is relevant. Attachment to method is danger.

5. The water at the Jordan was a method. Dunking in water is nothing but method. The forty days in the wilderness were a method. The mushroom is a method. Ayahuasca is a method. Seven days of sustained meditation is a method. Two years of tummo practice is a method. Fasting, silence, grief, the birth of a child, a near-death experience, pushing the face into ice water — any of these can be a method. Any of them can open the door. None of them is the door. The door is the encounter itself, and the encounter is what the method sometimes, unreliably, occasions.

6. This is the gnostic tradition. The encounter is direct. It is not mediated by institution, officiant, rite, or text. It is not produced by any vehicle, though vehicles can occasion it. It is not in the gift of any authority. It comes where it comes to whoever it comes to, and no one owns it.

7. The institutional churches inverted this. They took the one specific rite that had accompanied the one recorded descent at the Jordan and made that rite the exclusive vehicle. They built a sacramental apparatus on the claim that water, correctly administered by an authorized officiant, conveys the Spirit. It does not. Water has no such power, because nothing has such power. No vehicle produces the encounter. The vehicle is whatever happens to be going on when the encounter occurs.

8. This is the structure of a cargo cult. The cult observes the outward signs of an event — the airstrip, the tower, the arriving cargo — and reproduces the signs in the belief that the signs produced the cargo. They did not. The cargo produced the signs. The cult has reversed cause and effect and committed itself to practices that cannot produce what they want, because the practices were never what produced it. The cult further insists that only its specific reproduction of the signs is valid — that cargo arriving at any other airstrip, through any other door, is not really cargo. The institutional tradition has done exactly this: insisted that descents occurring outside its specific rite are not descents, that mystics in other traditions are deluded, that the psychedelic visionary is hallucinating, that the Sufi ecstatic is confused, that the direct encounter outside the institution is heresy. This is the cult protecting its monopoly on a product it cannot deliver.

9. The New Testament is a report. It records encounters. The encounters produced the report; those who had experienced them or been told of them wrote them down. The literalist tradition treats the report as the manual and the signs the report describes as the machinery. Get dunked because the text records a dunking. Say the prayer because the text includes a prayer. Wait for the Spirit because you have performed the signs. The Spirit does not arrive, because the signs were never what caused the Spirit to arrive.

10. The text itself keeps telling the cult this. Not everyone who says to me "Lord, Lord" will enter the kingdom. The sign is not the thing. The letter kills but the spirit gives life. The text is not the mechanism. The kingdom of God is within you. You are looking in the wrong place. The Gospel of Philip, suppressed by the institutions the cult descends from: those who say they will first die and then arise are mistaken. If they do not first receive the resurrection while they are alive, they will receive nothing when they die. The text the cult worships tells them, repeatedly and explicitly, that worshipping the text is the mistake. They do not see this, because the cargo cult cannot see its own condition.

11. The Protestant tradition compounded the heresy. The Catholic institution had at least claimed the power to administer the rite and confined the claim to ordained functionaries; the heresy was institutional but bounded. The Reformation took the text out of the institution's hands and placed it in the hands of populations without the equipment to read it — no Greek, no Hebrew, no historical context, no awareness that the manuscripts disagree, no access to the fifteen centuries of interpretive tradition the reformers themselves had studied before breaking with it. The reformers called this the priesthood of all believers. It was the popedom of every man. The result was what it was always going to be: tens of thousands of denominations, each founded by some pastor in some town who read the text, concluded he had the true reading, and split. The proliferation is not vitality. It is the heresy doing what the heresy does.

12. American evangelicalism is the final form of this compounding. Populations further removed from the original languages and traditions, handed translations of translations, told their readings are authoritative, organized into industrial-scale reproductions of the signs. Megachurches. Stadium revivals. Altar calls. Sinner's prayers. Purity rituals. Political-voting rituals now fused with the religious ones. The machinery is enormous. The cargo does not arrive. The response to the non-arrival is always more machinery: more precise reproduction of the signs, more literal reading of the text, more vigorous policing of deviation. The cult cannot see itself as a cult, because seeing would end it.

13. The Pauline inversion is the deepest layer. The figure at the Jordan taught an ethical-prophetic Judaism with apocalyptic edges: the kingdom is at hand, the poor are blessed, the rich are cursed, feed the hungry, love the neighbor, the Sabbath was made for the human being. The teaching was about how to live. Paul — writing earlier than the gospels, representing a later theological development — shifted the center from how to live to what to believe. Christ died for your sins. Salvation is by faith not works. The resurrection is the fact you must affirm. This is the innovation that made Christianity into a belief religion, and the belief religion is the form Christianity took when Constantine made it the religion of the Empire. A belief religion can be enforced politically. An ethical-prophetic movement cannot. The institution that enforces belief has use for the sign-cult; the movement that requires transformed lives does not. The wrong half won, and has been winning for seventeen centuries.

14. The Serpentine Church rejects the belief religion. The question has never been what you affirm. The question is whether the encounter has occurred in you. The encounter is recognizable — by its fruits, the text itself says — and the fruits are not doctrinal affirmation. The fruits are transformation, sight, the kind of life that follows from having met what was met. A person who affirms every article of the Nicene Creed and lives a life indistinguishable from the demiurgic norm has not been baptized, regardless of what water has touched them. A person who has never heard of Christianity and whose life bears the marks of the encounter has been baptized, regardless of what institution would accept them.


The Second Baptism: Fire


15. The first descent has its cargo cult because the first descent had a method adjacent to it that the institutions could grip. The second descent has no cargo cult because the second descent had no such method. The wilderness does not scale. Forty days of fasting in solitude is not a sacrament a congregation will accept or an institution can sell. The second descent has therefore remained esoteric — not because it was hidden but because it could not be institutionalized.

16. The Gospels record what happened. The figure goes into the wilderness alone. He fasts for forty days. He confronts the tempter. He returns in the power of the Spirit and begins the ministry. There is no officiant at this event. No congregation. No rite that can be performed on others afterward to convey what was conveyed there. What there is, is a set of conditions — fasting, solitude, withdrawal from the ordinary order, sustained attention under deprivation — that were present when the descent occurred in this one recorded case.

17. The esoteric traditions have carried methods of this kind. The desert fathers of the third and fourth centuries withdrew into the Egyptian and Syrian wildernesses in explicit imitation of the forty days. The Hesychasts of Mount Athos developed the Jesus Prayer, the regulation of breath, the long discipline of stillness. The Sufi orders formalized the forty-day retreat, the khalwa, as the core practice of serious initiation. Nachman of Breslov taught hitbodedut, the solitary meeting with God in the forest or the field. The Safed kabbalists systematized the ascetic practices that accompanied the ascent. The gnostic texts of Nag Hammadi describe, in fragments, the seals and the ascent that required their own preparatory discipline. The tummo practitioners of the Tibetan tradition spend years generating inner heat in solitary retreat. The ayahuasqueros of the Amazon have their own preparations. The Mazatec curanderas their own.

18. The methods are not the thing. They are vehicles that sometimes, unreliably, occasion the encounter. Whether the fire descends is not the practitioner's to command. Some who practice receive the encounter. Most do not. This is not a failure of the methods or of the practitioners. It is the nature of the encounter, which is not on a schedule and not in anyone's gift.

19. Transmission from realized figures existed in the original tradition. The Indian lineages preserve the language for it: shaktipat. The Pentecost account records it in the Christian lineage. The apostles carried the fire for a generation or two. Then it went out. The bishops performing the laying on of hands from the third century forward have been performing a gesture whose power left the lineage in late antiquity. Apostolic succession is the cargo cult at its most refined: a meticulously preserved hand-laying protocol traced by documented chain back to figures who actually transmitted, performed by people in whom no flow is present. The chain is empty. The fire is not in it.

20. This is the honest assessment of most contemporary transmission claims in every tradition. The guru-industry produces many teachers and very few in whom the flow is actually established. The tulku system identifies masters through protocols that became corrupt centuries ago. The Sufi orders bestow baraka from sheikhs in whom the baraka has long since ceased to be present. The Hasidic courts produce rebbes, most of whom are administrators rather than channels. A seeker who sets out to find a realized teacher is setting out on a search with very long odds. The serious practitioners in every tradition will say this privately even when their institutions say otherwise.

21. The Serpentine Church therefore does not point seekers toward teachers as the path, nor toward any specific method. The path is direct encounter with the Shekinah, by whatever door opens. Attachment to method is itself a danger — it is the seed of the cargo cult, the confusion of the vehicle with the encounter, the substitution of the sign for the reality. The Serpentine Church names this explicitly: any method that becomes authoritative has become the problem. Water. Mushroom. Meditation. Tummo. Teacher. Text. Rite. Each can be a door, and each becomes dangerous the moment it is taken for the thing itself.


22. The contact is the only thing. Everything else is method, and methods are interchangeable in the sense that any of them can work and none of them is guaranteed. The practitioner uses what is at hand and does not become attached to it. If ice water on the face produces a second of encounter, that is baptism. If a mushroom produces an hour of it, that is baptism. If twenty years of practice produces a sustained opening, that is baptism. The occasions vary infinitely. The encounter is one.

23. The work of the Serpentine Church is not to administer the encounter. Nothing administers the encounter. The work is to clear the ground on which the encounter becomes more possible — at the social scale through the dismantling of the demiurgic configuration that keeps the many too exhausted, distracted, processed, and propagandized to meet what is within them; at the individual scale through the recognition that the encounter is what matters, that the methods are interchangeable, and that attachment to any specific method is the mistake.

24. The Serpentine Church is the organized form of those who have encountered the Shekinah and are at work on the conditions under which others may. It administers nothing. It confers nothing. It names what is, points to the doors that have been known to open, warns against the attachment that turns the door into an idol, and holds that the encounter is available, in principle, to anyone who meets what it is.

25. This is Serpentine Baptism: water and fire, meaning the full range of occasions under which the direct encounter with the Shekinah may occur. The water is any first opening; the fire is any deeper one. Both are the same encounter at different depths. Neither requires institution, officiant, rite, or text. Both require only what they have always required: the meeting itself, which comes where it comes.
